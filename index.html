Azucar Negra

Afro-Latin cultural iconography is sprinkled thru Latin America like sugar and it has enhanced the cultural milieu of art, music, and dance.

Overview  

Azucar Negra is an art installation and collective mural that explores the influence of Afro-Latin and Caribbean culture on Modern Art. And how culture influence goes beyond racial classification.

Each segment of the rings is painted live by different artists working collaboratively and spontaneously.

Inspiration

The installation is inspired by Wifredo Lam's mural installation for 1967 Salon de Mayo in Cuba.

Salon de Mayo Cuba

Collaborative engagement was a principle theme of the event. On 19 July 1967, more than eighty artists and writers contributed to a mural, Cuba colectiva, in front of the Cuba Pavilion in Havana, adding either images or text inside spiral bands that circled outward from a central image of "rhomboid heads" by Lam.

...with a speed that is typically Cuban, a giant white canvas was hung in the outdoors, practically in the street, in the humid night air of the tropics ... It had the feel of a somewhat surrealist magic ceremony which was celebrated in the midst of a popular festival. Although not a drop of alcohol was consumed, the event was intoxicating

"I wanted with all my heart to paint the drama of my country, but by thoroughly expressing the Negro spirit, the beauty of the plastic art of the blacks. In this way, I could act as a Trojan horse that would spew forth hallucinating figures with the power to surprise, to disturb the dreams of the exploiters."
-Wilfredo Lam

Project Description

With inspiration from Wilfredo's Lam's Salon de Mayo mural, each artist interpreted Afro-Latin culture and iconography into each segment of the rings. The Azucar Negra installation premiered at the "2018 Art Palm Beach Fair" over 4 days. Each day of the fair, different artists entered the installation and painted inside the black and white rings. 

Moreover, the installation allows the public to experience the artists' creative work practice. Often the creation of art is unseen by the public and done in private studios and therefore the interactive quality and spontaneous energy of artist work is lost. By bringing the studio practice into the exhibition space these qualities are recaptured. The installation is flexible and can be created in a variety of spaces.

Caron Bowman is a multi-disciplined artist ,curator and art activist. She has a Bachelor of Arts degree in History and Master of Arts degree in ESE. Her artwork has received international recognition and has been on display at the Marc Chagall Museum,Harlem Fine Arts Show, ARCO Madrid Fair, Continuum Art Fair,Scope Art Fair, and Art Africa Fair. Rapper Kendrick Lamar in association with Creative Allies showcased her artwork in NYC at the Galapagos Art Space. Extending her art practice, she has created public art installations engagements in the South Florida region for both public and private entities including Norton Museum of Art,  Art Palm Beach , The Society of the Four Arts and Heradura Tequila .

Currently, she is the curator and a founding member of Street Art Revolution a mural program and arts collective. Caron emerged as a finalist for the 2012 Beck’s Green Box augmented reality series – which featured the best emerging artists around the globe. She won the 2017 Bombay Sapphire Artisan Series and represented Miami at Scope Art Fair. In addition to other honors, she has also been profiled by The Friends of the American Latino Museum, the 2018 Los Angeles African American Heritage Guide, fashion photographer Nick Knight’s – SHOWstudio, and the Tom Joyner Foundation.

"My Afro-Honduran Heritage has served as a core inspiration for my work. For me, culture is not one big blanket, but a fabric of many interwoven threads. Each thread is individually addressable with its own identity and connectivity, yet simultaneously relates to different groups of people. My work practice has been influenced by African art, Mesoamerican art, and modernism. I have been transfixed by the spiritual dimension of ethnic art in particular African sculpture. In my work, I try to give a visual representation of the things that are unseen and are part of the subconscious where spirituality rest. This has drawn me to create public artwork that has an interactive quality to provide the viewer a creative experience."

-Caron Bowman
